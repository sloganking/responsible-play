# Sight vs Punishment

The book's central stance: **you fix blindness with sight, not punishment.**

---

## The Argument

Most harm comes from people who can't see what they're doing. Punishing them doesn't fix the blindness — it often deepens it (they become defensive, enter [[narrative-lock]], or develop [[sinsickness]]). Showing them what they can't see has a chance of actually changing the pattern.

> Anyone can see wrongdoing, assume malice, and punish. That takes nothing — just righteous certainty and anger. Seeing wrongdoing and understanding why it happened, then helping the person find a better way — that takes empathy, courage, and everything you have. The first one feels like justice. The second one IS justice.

---

## The Caveat: Sight Without Wisdom Is a Weapon

Clear sight without trust is just a sharper weapon. "Trust doesn't care who's correct" — you can be right about someone's pattern and still cause harm by naming it without rapport, consent, or timing. Coach, not Inquisitor.

---

## What Clear Eyes Are For

Not inquisition. Not correction of every person you meet. Sight is for creating good — helping people who want help, building spaces that work, preventing harm through awareness rather than enforcement.
