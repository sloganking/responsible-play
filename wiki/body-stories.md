# Body Stories

Trauma stored in the body as automatic meaning-making patterns below conscious awareness. Not memories you recall — patterns that fire before conscious thought.

---

## What Trauma Actually Is

Trauma is a **story stored in the body.** When something in the present resembles something from the past — even vaguely — the trauma pattern activates. The body floods with stress hormones. The thinking brain goes offline. One of four automatic responses takes over:

| Response | Behavior | Story Running |
|----------|----------|--------------|
| **Fight** | Aggression, pushing back | "I must destroy the threat" |
| **Flight** | Leaving, avoiding | "I must escape the threat" |
| **Freeze** | Going still, dissociating | "If I don't move, maybe it stops" |
| **Fawn** | People-pleasing, agreeing | "If I make them happy, I'm safe" |

---

## When the Feeling Comes First

Normal path: Experience → Story → Feelings

Trauma path: Experience → **Feelings** (from implicit memory) → **Conscious Story** (mind invents cause) → More Feelings

The feeling arrives first. The conscious mind doesn't know it's from the past — it instantly constructs a present-tense cause. You don't experience yourself making up an explanation. You just *see* the person as dangerous.

---

## Where Body Stories Come From

1. **Direct experience** — Something happened to you
2. **Secondhand transmission** — Your mother was hurt by men; you absorbed her fear as your own
3. **Cultural inheritance** — An entire culture teaches a type of person is dangerous; the fear seeps in through thousands of small messages

All three produce the same result. Your body doesn't distinguish between fear you earned and fear you inherited.

---

## Key Story Patterns and Their Damage

| Story | Distortion | Mistake It Causes |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| "I'm always being left out" | Sees exclusion everywhere | Misreads situations, creates boundary violations from confusion |
| "Everyone wants what I have" | Paranoia about partner | Accuses innocent people of predatory behavior |
| "Facilitators are perfect" | Any staff error = predation | Over-responds to normal human mistakes |
| "I would never do that" | Can't imagine unconscious mistakes | Blind to own risk; crucifies others for accidents |

---

## "I Would Never Do That"

The person who thinks they'd never make a mistake is the most dangerous — both to themselves (no precautions) and to everyone they might judge (no mental category for "unconscious accident"). They become the loudest person in the room when something goes wrong.
