# Sinsickness

Your moral immune system attacking you. Shame that attacks **identity** rather than **behavior** — "I AM bad" instead of "I DID something bad."

---

## Mechanism

When you make a mistake — especially one that contradicts your self-image — your moral system can flip from healthy guilt ("I did something wrong, I need to fix it") to identity-level shame ("I am fundamentally broken"). The shame paralyzes repair because the person collapses into self-punishment instead of taking action.

Brené Brown's distinction: **Guilt** = "I did something bad." **Shame** = "I am bad." Guilt motivates repair. Shame motivates hiding, withdrawal, or self-destruction.

---

## How It Shows Up

- Withdrawing from community (self-exile as self-punishment, not accountability)
- Inability to take repair actions because "nothing I do can fix what I am"
- The dark feelings described in [[fawning]] → healing fawning — rage, intrusive thoughts, disgust at yourself
- A language gap making it worse: if the only words for what happened are "someone called me a predator," your own moral immune system attacks you

---

## The Language Connection

Having the word "[[righteous-predator]]" changes what you can survive. If someone falsely accuses you and the only language you have is "someone called me a predator," your sinsickness fires — *maybe they're right.* But "a righteous predator falsely accused me" makes the experience coherent. You're less likely to attack yourself over someone else's distortion.

---

## Sinsickness Spreads

When someone believes certain desires deserve shame, they shame anyone who has those desires. The shame voice gets installed in others. Now they're [[fawning]] to an internalized critic — arguing with a ghost about whether they deserve what they want.

---

## The Antidote: Compass Stories

Collect stories — movie clips, quotes, moments from books and films — that touch something real in you. Scenes about doing the right thing when it's hard. When the dark feelings try to tell you who you are, the stories remind you who you actually are.

> The heart that's horrified by what you're feeling IS your compass. The fact that you're disturbed by these feelings is the proof that they don't define you.

---

## Guilt as Self-Indulgence

When guilt makes you withdraw — stop contributing, stop showing up, stop creating value — it becomes self-indulgent. The world loses your contribution. Productive guilt drives repair and return. Collapsed guilt drives exile and stagnation. The distinction matters: what you did ≠ who you are.
