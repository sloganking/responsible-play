# Drama Triangle

Stephen Karpman's model of three reactive roles people fall into during conflict: **Victim**, **Persecutor**, **Rescuer**. The healthy alternatives (David Emerald's *The Empowerment Dynamic*): **Creator**, **Challenger**, **Coach**. The engine underneath the entire book.

---

## The Three Roles

Three sentences, one person, ten seconds:

> "I couldn't have done anything about it." ← **Victim**
> "He's an evil, selfish asshole who needs to be punished." ← **Persecutor**
> "Someone needs to stop him before he hurts anyone else." ← **Rescuer**

**Victim** — Doesn't take responsibility. Waits to be saved. The Victim position isn't actually powerless — it has enormous power to mobilize Rescuers and justify Persecution.

**Persecutor** — Blames, attacks, punishes. Often a former Victim who flipped to anger.

**Rescuer** — Swoops in to fix/help/protect but keeps the Victim helpless. In this book, the most dangerous role: [[righteous-predator|Rescuers cause more harm than predators]].

The roles aren't stable — people rotate constantly: Victim → Persecutor ("It's YOUR fault!") → Rescuer ("I need to stop this!") → Victim ("After everything I did for you!"). That rotation keeps the drama alive.

## The Empowerment Shifts

| Drama Role | Empowered Version | The Shift |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| Victim | **Creator** | "This happened. What do I want to create now?" → [[responsibility-triad]] |
| Persecutor | **Challenger** | Holds accountable with care. Pushes growth, not punishment → [[over-and-under-response]] |
| Rescuer | **Coach** | Supports without saving. Asks questions instead of giving answers. Doesn't need to be needed. |
